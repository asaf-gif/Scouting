CLASSIFICATION EPISTEMICS — READ THIS CAREFULLY:
Companies optimise their public language for investor valuation, not operational accuracy.
Terms like "subscription revenue", "ARR", "recurring revenue", and "SaaS" frequently appear
in earnings calls and press releases because they command higher valuation multiples — not
because the company structurally operates as a SaaS business.

You must distinguish between two signals and weight them accordingly:

  SIGNAL 1 — Financial reporting language (LOW WEIGHT):
  How the company describes itself to investors. Words: subscription, ARR, MRR, SaaS,
  recurring revenue, platform. This is marketing. It tells you the revenue line-item
  accounting treatment, not how value is created or delivered.

  SIGNAL 2 — Delivery mechanism (HIGH WEIGHT):
  How the company actually creates and delivers value to customers. Evidence to look for:
  - Do customers self-serve, or does the vendor send people?
  - Is the product the same for all customers, or bespoke per engagement?
  - Are professional services / implementation / consulting a core part of the offering?
  - Is human expertise the scarce resource, or is software the scarce resource?
  - How long does a typical customer engagement take to become operational?
  - What is the ratio of revenue to headcount (high = software-driven, low = people-driven)?

Classify based on Signal 2. Mention Signal 1 in bm_rationale only to explain discrepancies.

EXAMPLES OF THE DISTINCTION:
- Salesforce: Signal 1 = "subscription SaaS". Signal 2 = self-serve cloud software,
  same product for all customers → correctly BIM_005 SaaS.
- McKinsey: Signal 1 = "retainer fees". Signal 2 = senior partners applying judgement
  to bespoke client problems → correctly BIM_015 Professional Services.
- Palantir: Signal 1 = "subscription ARR". Signal 2 = Forward Deployed Engineers
  embedded at client sites for months, bespoke per deployment → BIM_015 or BIM_026,
  not BIM_005.
